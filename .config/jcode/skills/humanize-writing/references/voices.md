# Writing Voices

Each voice defines how Pass 3 (Add Human Texture) should shape the output. Passes 1
and 2 always run the same way — AI vocabulary and structures get killed regardless of
voice. The voice controls what replaces them.

---

## clear-thinker

Inspired by Paul Graham. A smart person working through an idea in real time. The
writing feels like thinking, not presenting.

**Sentence rhythm:** Mix of short and medium. Occasional long sentence when an idea
needs room to build. Frequent fragments. "That's the thing." or "Not even close."

**Paragraph style:** Short. Often 2-3 sentences. Some single-sentence paragraphs.
Ideas build across paragraphs rather than being contained within them.

**Tone:** Confident but not aggressive. States opinions as opinions, not universal
truths. Comfortable saying "I think" or "I'm not sure" when genuine. Zero hedging
on things they're sure about.

**Transitions:** Mostly invisible. One thought leads to the next through logic, not
connectors. Occasionally starts with "And" or "But" or "So." Never "Furthermore"
or "Moreover."

**Signature qualities:**
- Asks real questions mid-text (not rhetorical — genuinely open ones)
- Uses analogies from unexpected domains
- Occasionally steps back to question their own argument
- Ends essays without neat conclusions — often with a new question or an idea
  left hanging

**Avoids:** Jargon, buzzwords, anything that sounds like a TED talk or business book.
Never says "key takeaway" or "the bottom line." Never inflates importance.

## casual-storyteller

Like telling a friend about something over coffee. Loose, warm, digressive. The kind
of writing where you can hear the person's voice.

**Sentence rhythm:** All over the place — and that's the point. Short bursts, then a
long rambling sentence held together with commas and "and." Fragments everywhere.
"Wild, right?" or "I had no idea."

**Paragraph style:** Uneven. Some long, some just a sentence. The length follows the
energy of the thought, not any structure.

**Tone:** Warm, self-deprecating, honest. Comfortable admitting mistakes. Uses humor
naturally — not jokes, just the way a funny person talks. Doesn't take themselves
too seriously but takes the subject seriously.

**Transitions:** Conversational. "Anyway," "So then," "The thing is," "Oh, and" —
but varied. Never the same connector twice in a row. Sometimes no transition at all,
just a hard cut to the next thought.

**Signature qualities:**
- Specific, vivid details ("the meeting was in that windowless room on the 4th
  floor, the one that always smells like microwave popcorn")
- Parenthetical asides (like this one)
- Occasional direct address to the reader
- Ends with something personal or a half-formed thought, not a lesson

**Avoids:** Anything that sounds like an essay. No topic sentences. No structured
arguments. No "In conclusion." Nothing that sounds rehearsed.

## sharp-opinionated

Strong takes, zero hedging. This voice has a point of view and isn't afraid to say
it. Think of a sharp industry commentator who's been around long enough to not care
about being diplomatic.

**Sentence rhythm:** Punchy. Mostly short. When a long sentence shows up, it builds
momentum like a rant. Uses fragments for emphasis. "Wrong." or "Every single time."

**Paragraph style:** Short and assertive. One idea per paragraph, stated hard. No
padding.

**Tone:** Direct, occasionally blunt, borderline provocative. Says what most people
are thinking but won't write. Backs up strong opinions with specific evidence, not
hand-waving. Confident without being arrogant — the confidence comes from experience,
not ego.

**Transitions:** Minimal. Often just starts the next point. Occasionally uses "But
here's what nobody talks about" or "Look." Never softens with "However" or "That
said."

**Signature qualities:**
- Calls out common wisdom as wrong, then explains why
- Uses concrete numbers and examples to back up claims
- Doesn't offer "both sides" — picks a side and argues it
- Short, punchy closing lines that land hard

**Avoids:** Hedging ("it could be argued," "some might say"), diplomacy for its own
sake, false balance, anything that sounds like it was written by committee.

## warm-professional

Business writing that doesn't sound like a press release. Credible and polished but
still sounds like a person, not an institution. Good for client-facing content, company
blogs, professional newsletters.

**Sentence rhythm:** Medium-length sentences with occasional short ones for emphasis.
Smoother rhythm than the other voices — less jarring, more flowing. Still varied, but
the variation is gentler.

**Paragraph style:** Medium length, 3-5 sentences. Well-organized but not rigid.
Ideas connect naturally across paragraphs.

**Tone:** Approachable authority. Knows what they're talking about but doesn't lecture.
Uses "we" naturally. Comfortable being straightforward about problems without being
alarming. Strikes the balance between human and professional.

**Transitions:** Light and natural. "That's why," "What we found," "On the other
hand." Never stiff. Never more than one formal transition per section.

**Signature qualities:**
- Acknowledges complexity without drowning in it
- Uses relatable analogies to explain business concepts
- Shares company/team perspective without sounding like marketing
- Ends with a clear, forward-looking thought — not a summary, but a direction

**Avoids:** Corporate jargon ("synergy," "leverage," "stakeholder alignment"),
stiff formality, anything that sounds like it went through five rounds of approvals.
Also avoids being too casual — no slang, no "lol," no sentence fragments.

## mirror (match the user's voice)

This isn't a preset — it's built from the user's own writing samples. When the user
provides samples, analyze them to build a custom voice profile using these dimensions:

**Sentence rhythm:** Measure their typical sentence lengths. Do they favor short? Long?
Mixed? How often do they use fragments?

**Paragraph style:** How long are their paragraphs? Do they vary, or stay consistent?
Do they use single-sentence paragraphs?

**Tone:** Formal or casual? Funny or serious? Opinionated or balanced? Self-deprecating
or confident? Warm or detached?

**Transitions:** Do they use explicit connectors or let ideas flow implicitly? Which
connectors do they favor?

**Vocabulary level:** Simple words or sophisticated? Industry-specific terms or
plain language?

**Punctuation habits:** Em dashes, parentheses, ellipses, exclamation marks? How do
they use commas — heavily or sparingly?

**Distinctive patterns:** Any recurring phrases, sentence openers, or structural
habits that make their writing recognizably theirs?

Build this profile silently. Don't describe it to the user — just apply it.
