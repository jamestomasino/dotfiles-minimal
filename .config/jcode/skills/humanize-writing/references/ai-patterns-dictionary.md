# AI Writing Patterns Dictionary

A comprehensive reference of known AI writing tells, compiled from Wikipedia's "Signs of AI Writing," Reddit communities, GPTZero research, and professional editing forums. Use this as a checklist when reviewing and rewriting text.

---

## 2. Banned Structural Patterns

These sentence/paragraph shapes are the strongest AI tells — even stronger than vocabulary.

### Stylistic Negation & Contrast Framing ("It's not X, it's Y" / "Not X, but Y")
**AI patterns:** "It's not X, it's Y." / "Not X, but Y." / "X isn't really X. It's Y." / "This isn't about X — it's about Y."
**Why it's a tell:** LLMs love setting up a contrast by denying one thing before stating another. It sounds rhetorical and deliberate when a human would just state the point directly. The negation-first frame appears far more often in AI text than in natural human writing.
**Fix:** Drop the contrast setup. Just state what is.
Bad: "The next phase of search isn't really search. It's synthesis."
Good: "The next phase of search is synthesis: a machine that reads..."
Bad: "It's not about rankings. It's about the corpus itself."
Good: "The contest is over the corpus itself."
Bad: "This isn't just a brand problem. It's a safety problem."
Good: "In a regulated category, this can be a safety problem, a compliance problem, and a trust problem."

### Meta-Framing (Announcing Intent Instead of Acting)
**AI patterns:** "Here's a way to explain..." / "There's a simple way to think about this..." / "Consider this analogy..." / "The analogy here is simple..." / "To understand X, think of..." / "What this means in practice is..." / "There's a temptation to dismiss..."
**Why it's a tell:** These sentences read like a message from the writer to themselves, not content for the reader. They announce intent instead of delivering substance. A human writer would just make the point or use the analogy without the preamble.
**Fix:** Drop the announcement. Just do the thing.
Bad: "Here's a way to explain the problem to a non-technical audience. Traditional search was like a librarian..."
Good: "Traditional search was like a librarian handing you a shelf of books. AI search is more like a junior associate..."
Bad: "The analogy here is simple. Ethical GEO is like improving the quality of a medicine label."
Good: "Ethical GEO is like improving the quality of a medicine label. Manipulative AEO is like slipping a flyer into the box."
Bad: "There's a temptation to dismiss all of this as just another version of SEO."
Good: "Old SEO was messy. This is different because..."

### Tricolon (Rule of Three)
**AI pattern:** "...research, collaboration, and problem-solving" / "vibrant, dynamic, and innovative"
**Why it's a tell:** LLMs default to groups of three adjectives, nouns, or phrases to sound comprehensive. Humans rarely write this way outside of speeches.
**Fix:** Pick the one or two that actually matter. Or just use a different sentence structure entirely.

### Em Dash Overuse
**AI pattern:** "The manager — who had been struggling — decided to act."
**Why it's a tell:** AI uses em dashes where humans would use commas, parentheses, or just split into two sentences. Wikipedia editors call this the "ChatGPT dash."
**Fix:** Use commas, periods, or parentheses. Reserve em dashes for rare emphasis only (max 1-2 per piece).

### The Rhetorical Question + Immediate Answer
**AI pattern:** "What does this mean? It means that..."
**Why it's a tell:** LLMs use this as a transition device constantly. Humans occasionally do it; AI does it every few paragraphs.
**Fix:** Just make the point directly.

### "Here's the thing:" / "Here's what nobody tells you:"
**AI pattern:** Starting paragraphs with dramatic reveals.
**Why it's a tell:** Overused LinkedIn/content-marketing AI pattern.
**Fix:** Drop the setup and start with the actual point.

### "Let's dive in" / "Let's unpack this" / "Let's break this down"
**AI pattern:** Invitation to explore.
**Why it's a tell:** Almost never appears in natural human writing. It's a podcast/YouTube intro structure.
**Fix:** Just start explaining.

### Neat Paragraph Endings
**AI pattern:** Every paragraph wraps up with a tidy conclusion or takeaway.
**Why it's a tell:** Humans leave loose ends, trail off, or let ideas hang. AI wraps everything in a bow.
**Fix:** Let some paragraphs just... end. Not every thought needs a landing.

### Mirror Structure (A/B Parallelism)
**AI pattern:** "A hands-on manager isn't micromanaging — they're firefighting. A distant manager isn't disengaged — they're trusting."
**Why it's a tell:** Perfect structural symmetry in consecutive sentences is an LLM favorite.
**Fix:** Break the symmetry. Make the second example structurally different from the first.

### Overuse of Colons for Reveals
**AI pattern:** "The result: a complete disaster." / "The outcome? Four managers agreed."
**Why it's a tell:** LLMs love the dramatic colon or question-mark reveal.
**Fix:** Weave it into a normal sentence.

### Inflation of Importance
**AI pattern:** "This represents a pivotal moment in..." / "This is a testament to..."
**Why it's a tell:** LLMs puff up the importance of everything. They rarely just state a fact without editorializing about how significant it is.
**Fix:** State facts. Let the reader decide if it's important.

## 3. Tone and Voice Tells

### Too Balanced / Too Fair
AI text gives equal weight to all perspectives. Real humans have opinions and lean into them.

### No Imperfections
Human writing has half-finished thoughts, slightly awkward transitions, sentences that start with "And" or "But," occasional fragments. AI text is uniformly polished.

### Absence of Personal Voice
AI text lacks the specific quirks (humor, sarcasm, frustration, tangents) that make writing feel human.

### Sycophantic / Over-Positive Tone
AI defaults to enthusiasm. "Great question!" "Absolutely!" "This is a fantastic approach!" Humans are more measured, skeptical, and sometimes blunt.

### Uniform Sentence Length
AI tends to write sentences of similar length throughout a piece. Humans naturally vary between short punchy sentences and longer meandering ones.

### Hedging Phrases
"Generally speaking," "to some extent," "from a broader perspective," "it could be argued that" — AI hedges constantly to appear balanced. Humans just say what they think.

## 4. Content-Level Red Flags

### Generic Conclusions
AI wraps up with vague, safe takeaways that could apply to almost anything: "Ultimately, what matters most is finding the right balance."

### No Concrete Details or Anecdotes
AI generates plausible-sounding but generic examples. Real human writing includes specific names, dates, places, numbers, and "you had to be there" details.

### Regression to the Mean
AI smooths specific facts into generic statements. Where a human might say "we lost $40k on that campaign," AI says "the initiative faced financial challenges."
